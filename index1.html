    IT Work

    • Please tell us about your IT work. What exactly do you do?

      My name’s Wolf, I have an IT business in a small country town roughly three hundred kilometres from Melbourne. I run the business as a sole trader, I have a part-time employee and a casual employee. I’ve been running the business for twenty-five years, I started off in the IT industry from the cabling aspect, doing telephone and network cabling for many years, until my curiosity got the better of me and I got the opportunity to do some work with servers and have since branched out into all sorts of IT industry. The cabling aspect was the most valuable part of my business as we were heading into the new century, convergence was really starting to come in on its own, telephones, faxes, VoIP and IoT were all converging and everything needed to be cabled or run by Wi-Fi. The cabling aspect taught me a lot about how things are interconnected. I work with a small number of core customers that I manage, ranging from manufacturing to retail and the hospitality industry. I also have worked for federal, state, and local governments, in either procuring hardware refreshes on behalf of their IT people. Being so far from Melbourne, a lot of companies and federal/state government agencies don’t usually have the resources to send people out to small country towns, so they usually find someone locally to work for them. Their work is very varied, which takes me into unknown areas that I’m expected to understand all their requirements.

    • Please tell us about the industry you work in.

      You need to have an understanding of old and new technology, not every industry is up to the challenge of keeping up to date, some of the strategies in your research and development are to maintain some of the old information as your clients are unable to meet a changing environment. Some clients are unable to keep up with the changes that occur, and so you have to maintain a link to the past with the technology they have. It’s not like you move forward and forget the past, you’re bound by your customers to have a link with old technology. So basically, you are a GP in a country town for the IT industry, but then a requirement comes such as a business upgrading. You suddenly have to become a specialist. You then provide specialist support then go back to general work again. There’s a constant evolution of meeting new things and overcoming them. It’s quite a challenge for an individual to be able to do that. Whereas a larger organisation can have those resources at hand and meet those ever-changing needs. Many times, I have considered just working at a factory and just doing work that is less challenging and dangerous. Dangerous in the sense of your mental health, you are given the keys to the safety of your businesses so there’s a lot of responsibility there if your business fails due to your carelessness. The association and trust with your client are paramount. If there’s a disaster caused by your negligence, your mental health will suffer from it, even if it is caused by other means beyond your control. You’ll look over the scene and try to determine whether it was your fault. It may not have been, it may have just been carelessness, may have been a ransomware attack. The pressure in the IT industry when you’re self-employed is that everything hangs on that. If you’re not good at your work, your business will fail. You are employed by your merits, you’re not like a salesman selling things, but you’re actually selling your services and skills. This applies especially in a small country town, if you’re not good at IT word gets around.

    • What other kinds of work do you have to do?

      Some of the other work I do are for companies and government agencies that require remote work. It generally includes a brief that’s sent to me and I just go out and carry out their instructions, whether it be an NBN installation, a security camera installation/configuration, or a hardware refresh. There’s always something that goes wrong and you need to have the skills to work with your clients and resolve the situation if possible. There’s a lot of skills to be learned, whether it be working with IT help desks overseas. That’s a real challenge to get the message across due to language barriers; you’ll need to use all the skills and tricks you can such as talking with photos. You also need to resolve the situation fast because these big companies don’t want to spend too much time or money on these short jobs that they commission. I prefer to have my loyal customers and go my own way, I’m not really keen on working with contractors. Unfortunately, we’re the soldiers in the trenches, everyone up the top gets their nice fat paycheck and we have to negotiate, and we’re screwed for hourly rates so it’s not a pleasant place to be. In a country town, you need your business to survive so there’s no choice but to take the work.

    • Who are all the different people you interact within your work? Please tell us about them.

      The types of people I interact with are clients from the food industry, wholesalers, manufacturing, local industries such as custom-made logging trailers and load trailers for construction work. It’s really interesting to be a part of a team where I’m ensured that all the IT infrastructure operates successfully, a lot of the machinery is computer controlled, it’s really important to keep everything functioning and have an understanding of how things work because in IT it’s not always just programming, sometimes it involves repairing a plasma cutter and the computer inside that’s failed, you’ve never seen a plasma cutter before nor a customised computer before, so you just got to go in and fix it. While there are spare parts available, sometimes they can’t be sourced and quite often factories can’t afford that downtime. This is where it comes down to your general IT skills and understanding of processors. Another one is a timber merchant, where they process quality flooring, wall paneling and decking. Again, these are more like basic computer-controlled machines, not like your gaming console, these machines control basic repetitive processes. In one instance, one of the controllers failed and the replacement controller had to be built overseas since there weren’t any parts available so there was a possible downturn of four million dollars. And again, this is where your general skills come in, such as knowing what electronic components are and if they are damaged. You learn to keep your good customers; you get rid of your bad customers when you can afford to stand up and pay your bills. Unfortunately, when you start up in business you have no choice, you have to take everything you can get until you get yourself established. I like to spend more and more time with my clients and with the help of some of my colleague’s management tools, it helps me free up some time; they serve a valuable part in my operations now. Also, as I’m quite senior in my life, a handover to my colleague will be quite easy as we’re using all of his tools to manage my clients. It’s always been a concern that if I’m no longer able to look after my clients anymore, what will happen to them if I’m taken ill? Will they be left to the sharks or is there some plan in place? Again, it all comes down to the collegiate groups, you pick up something, you find someone that’s doing similar things and then you start discussing how you can help each other, and you move on.

    • Please tell us about your interactions with other IT professionals.

      In the early years of doing cabling work, running cables was a no brainer, but it's what the cable does that always intrigued me i.e. “What does it transport?”. In the eighties and nineties, there was no google and your information was very hard to come by, other than libraries and bookstores. In terms of being a self-learner, it’s a really hard path. As the internet started to take shape, internet forums became available. One of my suppliers recommended that I seek out some discussion groups along with my pursuits and that’s where I started to communicate with similar minded people. I found that had I not done that; I would have fallen behind in terms of being able to increase my knowledge. You need to have some sort of ability to connect with people who know more than you do and are on a similar path of self-discovery and a desire for helping out people. Once I found a really good group, which was the small business user group in the late nineties, I never looked back. That evolved into a SMBIT group which is quite large today. Without the association, I would be quite isolated in a country town as I wouldn’t have the knowledge just by searching on my own. When you belong to an association you get far more exposure to cutting edge technology. Quite often in country towns are a few years behind their city counterparts in terms of technology reaching them. You don’t have the ability to interact with the latest technology, because your customers aren’t interested, and you don’t have the finances to be able to buy yourself a 36 terabyte NAS drive and work with the storage and backups. I found by having an association with IT professionals, you make friends in the cyberworld and instances where you’re getting a job and having to walk into an unknown area or breaking something without being fully aware of what you’ve done, you still got this collegiate group of people that you can contact and they’ll help you out of a possible mess you’ve made or give you advice on what you should provide to the customer. If you don’t have that collegiate group you’re really on your own island, you need people that can provide you support in a professional way. I was fortunate enough to meet someone that was providing some IT services in my area and we were both members of the IT professionals organisation. Now we work together, where he has a larger client base, has all the remote management tools and experience in knowing how to improve my services towards my clients. It’s been really positive having that opportunity to know someone that I ordinarily wouldn’t ever meet. I would just be this little person in a country town not understanding any new technology or knowing what to look for. Just by sitting back and reading discussions amongst professionals, occasionally there will be something that’s discussed that’s right up your alley, it’s worth belonging to an organisation just for this reason.

    • What about your interactions with clients or investors?

      Interactions with my customers is a very personal one, that’s due to just being in a country town. The relationship is sincere, it has to be one hundred percent honest that I provide them with the best knowledge that I have and inform them that I don’t have the leading edge on items, but I can still offer a few pathways to choose from as we’re meeting all the new changes. That happens in all sorts of industries whether it’s the tourist industry, individuals or community groups that I volunteer, so it’s important that you have some stake where you provide your services back into the community for organisations in need of IT services that can’t afford to pay the larger companies. I don’t see myself as a salesperson although IT can sometimes be a perfect opportunity for salespeople to move into. I see myself more as a friend and a provider of knowledge to equip a business to run successfully. What makes me fairly unique is that I come from a background with a number of trades and experiences in quite a number of areas. When I interact with my clients, I’ll often have an experience I can relate to and that takes me down a path of showing an interest in my clients and honesty that I understand what they’re doing in order to provide the correct IT advice for their future development. Whilst I moved into a lot of remote support work for my clients, I still feel it is essential that I make contact with as many people I can, whether it’s the CEO right down to the worker in the factory. I try and maintain contact and friendships inside the business, allowing me to get an idea of whether the company seems to be operating smoothly. Because quite often if that’s not done, you’ll find some worker’s IT equipment not functioning correctly and it’s left unaddressed. By the time you’re made aware of the issue, it’s blown up and caused anxiety amongst a number of people. So that’s why you get friendly with as many staff as you can and treat the situation as if you’re a part of the business and that they’re free to contact you when there’s an issue, unlike a managed service agreement or an impersonalised ticket which my colleague has in place. He has about ten times more clients than I have, he just cannot provide that personal touch. Yes, it’s all part of business as that’s the way he operates, but I didn’t want to go down that path. I just cannot see myself taking more customers and having less time to interact with the shop floor. That’s just how I wanted it, I don’t believe that in order to be a successful business you have to constantly expand and make more profit. There’s a sustainable way of operating an IT business and that’s a part of my belief that I always held; I wouldn’t expect someone to do something that I wouldn’t be prepared to do myself. That can also work the other way round as my customers can see how I operate, I’m happy to not run in this headlong ‘success is by excess’ mentality. They can see that I’m not taking them down a black hole of thousands of dollars each time I have an idea. The idea is to provide them with working technology that’s fit for purpose, not technology that’s not required and is just part of a profit-making operation.

    • Please tell us about your IT work. What exactly do you do?

      Quite often you’re not going to work with modern equipment where everything has spare parts with twenty-four seven productions running smoothly because you have stock of every single fallible item. It just doesn’t happen in the real world ever. People just rely on the infrastructure to work and when it fails, when you take action. Questions like “Well how did this happen?” and “Why didn’t we have a backup or a spare part?” leads to changes in the pattern of how IT is thought of in the industry. In some instances, machinery built thirty to fifty years ago is still present in the industry, that’s where your knowledge has to come in. There’s no retrofitting of some machinery, so often you’ll either have to fix it or replace it totally and that’s a huge investment for the company. For an IT professional, if you have really good general skills, you’ll make yourself valuable in the business sector. In country towns like this word gets around pretty quickly that some have saved a company millions of dollars from a disaster, so it’s a great asset to have.

    • Which aspects of your work do you find most challenging?

      The things I find most challenging in the IT world is balancing family and your personal life with workload demands. That’s always been a difficult thing to do in terms of not knowing when there’s a failure from a managed monitoring system, not knowing when you have to suddenly leave home and have to go on site. There’s also a concern about all the changes in the IT world and threats security. Trying to stay one step ahead of hackers, having to understand the law in terms of cyber breaches. There are all these challenges that are constantly running in the background, whether it be working on your ability to try and find time to shut everything out or have some personal space is almost impossible. There’s no way out of this system because you can’t control what happens in cyberspace, it’s moving and you’ve got to move with it, there’s very little breathing space. The other challenge is running a business and getting your invoices out to stay afloat. You need to also have in mind how you want your business to evolve and ensure you are successful in keeping your clients functioning with the right balance of IT equipment. You also need to look at your own strengths and weaknesses and watch out for threats that might come upon you, whether it’s your health or a fire breaks out. You also need to have a contingency and backup plan to ensure you’ve prepared something for yourself.

    • Finally, can you share an example of the work you do that best captures the essence of the IT industry?

      My clients are so varied, I really haven’t got one example that fits. All I can do is talk about one client that would give you an insight into the challenges I had to meet. It was a local steel merchant around ten years ago who was a part of a strong Christian community and initially needed some IT support. Their IT knowledge was very limited, they were very wary of the potential influence technology could impact on their community. There was a point where they had to move a paper-based operation into an electronic operation. The challenge there was to gain their trust, respect their beliefs and ensure that I would protect them as best I could from outside world influences and ensure that they were protected from the online world that they were connecting to. That meant finding a suitable software program that would manage their accounting and manufacturing, and research what was available gave good support and had similar values that I had in terms of respecting this religious group and also work closely with the owners in customising the program to suit their needs. These people only recently used mobile phones, so it was a real challenge to get them up to speed in terms of being able to operate a business in an electronic form away from ledgers. Over the years, they’ve gained an understanding of IT architecture, such as servers, switches, networking, and are now rolling out security camera systems for their organisation, even to the ever-changing threat from the cyberworld and having to take them through the ten years of evolving technology. They’ve adapted quite well and that’s basically where I’ve learned a lot of my skills in being able to follow a client through from the start like that. It requires a whole range of patience and slow steps with a long-term vision. Most other clients didn’t have that setback, so working with this client really needed some thinking outside the box on how you’re going to take a client into the digital world in a safe and respectful way so that they can be successful in a world that is all changing electronically.

    Cyber Security :-

    Cyber-attacks are known to happen every 39 seconds by an unknown hacker around the world according to a Clark school study at the University of Maryland. Since hackers are focused on attacking computers with internet access, there is a need to protect electronic systems, networks, computers, mobile devices, programs and mainly data from these malicious attacks performed by them. This is where the need of Cyber Security is required, as it is known as the practice of defending the devices that have internet access from such hacks. A strong cybersecurity system has multiple layers of protection spread across computers, devices, networks and programs. Cybercriminals can deploy a variety of attacks against individual victims or businesses which includes getting access, making changes or deleting sensitive and confidential data which in-turn interferes with business processes. In-order to prevent this, Cyber security can be achieved via three different components in an infrastructure which includes Information Technology security - protects the data when it's stored or while transferring the data through a network enabling IT security to protect both digital and physical data from the intruders, Cyber security - considered to be a subset of IT security which protects digital data on user's networks, computers, and devices from unauthorised access and prevents destruction to take place, Network security - also known as computer security which is a subset of cyber security that uses hardware and software to protect any data that is sent via one user's computer and other devices to the network. Network security also acts as a guard against data being changed or stolen by cybercriminals. There are different types of cyber threats including Social Engineering - way of manipulating people psychologically and forcing them into perform malicious activities or forcing to give away information, APTs (Advanced Persistent Threats) - break through a network anonymously and staying for a long period of time, Malware - designing a specific software to gain access or damage a computer without the knowledge of the owner, that can be protected with these cyber security methods. Other methods of cyber security include Application security, Cloud security, Data loss prevention, End-user education, critical infrastructure security etc.

    The state of cyber security 2020 :

    The cybersecurity skills gap has been plaguing enterprises for years, but despite garnering much discussion and media coverage, little progress is being made. On top of that challenge, cyberattacks continue to increase and are often underreported.

    Some of the latest cyber security methods includes :-

    Virtual Dispersive Networking (VDN) -that helps in tackling MiM attacks which cracked traditional encryptions and attacked the intermediate nodes. This innovation helps in splitting a message into numerous parts which is then encrypted and is routed into several different protocols on independent paths which leaves the hacker scrambling in-order to find the data that rolls in dynamically optimum paths as they whip through data centres, the cloud etc.

    Smart Grid Technologies -this technology uses a range of new security standards and measures such as padlock - which is a gateway that establishes encrypted communication between the central systems and the field devices, The Watchdog - performs a deep inspection on data packets for the control system

    SAML and the Cloud -this technology helps in encompassing an encryption with SAML and detects any intrusions in-order to regain control of corporate traffic. By doing this, the data available in the cloud is corralled and firms will be signalled by the alert system if any issues persist like unexpected logins, suspicious activities, etc.

    Future of Cyber security:

    1. Artificial Intelligence (AI): AI is used to crunch data, automate complex tasks, and make faster decisions than a human. Since more enterprises are dependant on machine learning, hackers will definitely target on AI systems for which the cybersecurity developers will be forced to develop cybersecurity software techniques to detect any corruption attacks that would take place on AI for which the developers of cybersecurity will themselves use AI to address any vulnerabilities and issues with regards to security before they can be taken advantage of, and will repel cyberattacks once it has begun. For this to happen, the developers in particular, will have to embed AI in user interfaces to warn the users about websites that might provide potential risks or any security choices that have been poorly chosen. The Artificial Intelligence technology may also be used in-order to create a simulated network attack which helps in revealing any weak points so they can be solved.

    2. Cyber-warfare: Cyber-warfare is where one nation tries to embed viruses in the computer systems of another nation that might become a way in which wars will be fought all around the world. Cybersecurity will have to find ways to make key pieces of infrastructure, for those which would make appealing targets during a cyberwar, more flexible to digital intrusions by adding multiple layers of security to traffic systems.

    3. Improvising on cybersecurity talents and developing talented cybersecurity developers will become essential as it will be expensive for organisations to hire a security analyst and the need will become desperate due to improvement in technology especially after emergence of 5G network. Thus, the organisations will try to train with ethical hacking courses such as Cybrary which enables IT professionals to become white hat hackers. etc...which in-turns increase the job opportunities for cybersecurity students.

    Possible changes due to technological improvements that defines the next 10 years of global cybersecurity are:

    1. 5G network and infrastructure convergence: This technology will pose a most challenging issue for the cybersecurity landscape. a 5G network will impact the threat landscape as it potentially moves cybercrime from being an invisible financial driven issue to one where serious physical damage that might occur at a 5G pace as the network provides instant access to any attacker to vulnerable networks.

    2.Artificial Intelligence: Though AI is an important component that helps the world to build immunity from attacks, it poses various new challenges to cybersecurity as any technical developments in AI will be quickly seized upon and will be exploited by the cybercriminals as they use AI for generating new attack cycles that are designed to target high which will create greatest impact in new ways that had never been experienced by the industries before.

    3.Biometrics: Biometric authentication requires high volumes of information about an individual including their voices, faces, and even the slightest details of movement and behavioural traits in next-generations which drives the cybercriminals to target and hack those data.

    Clouds, services, servers:-

    What does it do?

    Cloud computing is the delivery of computing services which includes from applications to storages and processing power over the internet to offer faster and flexible resources. Cloud computing includes servers, storage, databases, networking and software. It allows for data processing to be done on a privately-owned cloud or a third-party server. This creates maximum speed and reliability. Cloud computing works by offering the businesses to hire anything from application to storages from a cloud service provider instead of buying it. The main benefit of it is that the firms can avoid the upfront cost of buying the services as it is expensive to own, and the firm can also avoid the maintenance cost of it. Other benefits of cloud computing are ease of installation and scalability. This way it grows with your needs.

    Cloud computing offers three most common types of cloud services. They are:

    1) Software as a service (SaaS) –Packed Software OS & Application Stack Server Storage Network

    2) Platform as a service (PaaS) –OS & Application Stack Server Storage Network

    3) Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS) – Server Storage Network

    The above services depend on the requirement of a user and they can be used individually or with the combination of others. SaaS provides various software to the user which can be hired. Whereas, PaaS provides a platform for users where they can host, implement, test or run an application. However, IaaS provides a virtualized platform where the user can create a virtual machine monitor or hypervisor. Furthermore, the user can develop and install their own operating systems, software and applications.

    A cloud server is a virtual server running in a cloud computing environment. It is built, hosted and delivered via a cloud computing platform via the online interface, and can be accessed remotely. They are also known as virtual servers. The main features of cloud servers are that it is cheap compared to actual servers, and it can be easily expandable as it depends on the users and it is much more secure than the physical server.

    Blockchain :-

    What does it do?

    The Blockchain is the latest and prospective technology in the current economy. This technology can help to solve various kinds of problems in the industrial area, such as security, transparency, trust, and reliability of data processing.A blockchain is a structure of scattered data that is copied and shared among the members of a network. Each block within the chain carries a list of transactions and a hash to the previous block.Blockchains are copied across a peer-to-peer network, the data they have is very hard to corrupt or extinguish. With a blockchain in place, applications that previously run only through a trusted intermediary, now can function in a decentralized fashion, without a central authority, and attain the similar functionality with the same amount of certainty. Blockchain enables trust less networks, as the parties can transact even, they do not trust each other.The consensus mechanism is an important feature of a blockchain as it makes sure that all participants of a distributed ledger are on the same page and enables the network to keep running even if few members are failing. The most used consensus mechanism is the Proof-Of-Work protocol used by the first-ever cryptocurrency, bitcoin.

    A digital currency created from code to use as a method of exchange is known as Cryptocurrency. Traditional banking and Government systems have no function in it. Blockchain allows to operate Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies without the need for a central authority by spreading its operations across a network of computers. By using this, risks are going to be reduced and processing and transaction fees will be eliminated. The heavy use of cryptography is a main characteristic of blockchain networks which brings authoritativeness behind all the interactions in the network. The technology is still innovative, but its potential is vast. Lots of research and development ideas are in practical tests.

    In the next three years, four blockchain trends can be seen:

    1) Non-fungible tokens (NFTs)An NFT is a token on the Ethereum blockchain that carries unique metadata that separates it from other tokens. NFT’s can be used to store much more complex and individual-specific information, while currency is fungible and can be easily transferred.

    2) Security tokensSecurity tokens are cryptographic, programmable securities that function as an asset that can be used to take actions. Security tokens can pay interest, dividends, or invest in other assets or tokens, along with other functions.

    3) Tokenized assetsOver the next three years, Traditional lack of liquidity in assets will be overcome by new forms of liquidity as security tokens, such as real estate or art.

    4) Self-sovereign identityAs cyberattacks continue to multiply, new forms of identity verification will come over the scene to protect user’s information. Self-sovereign identity will allow users to keep a single digital identity across various platforms while choosing the information they want to share. This type of interaction would significantly transform the current digital marketplace that has converted personal data into a commodity.

    What went well in Group?

    In order to carry out our assignment activities effectively and efficiently, the main aspects which went well among our group was: Delegation of work, time-management, and cooperation. Through using Microsoft Teams, each meeting would have an agenda where we would read over the assignment specification and understand what work was needed to be completed. In our team, we divided our work into subsections where some members would focus on specific tasks, e.g. designing the website, rewriting the document, finalising the documents for submission, etc. Regarding time management, our team was consistent in working from start to finish, there weren’t any instances of last-minute rushed work, as we set due dates for each section prior to the actual due date of submission. Lastly, cooperation was another key strength of our group. There were no cases of disagreement or arguments over the work set out for each member, we were all content on completing the work assigned to us.

    What could be improved?

    The main aspect needing improvement was our communication skills. Some members in our group don’t excel in public speaking and had a bit of trouble conveying their progress on the assignment. As our group members come from diverse backgrounds, language barriers may contribute to this. However as a whole, we believe we can overcome this problem together in our future assignments and work towards addressing our common goal of delivering work to a high standard.

    At least one thing that was surprising.

    The main thing that surprised our group was the amount of dedication that each and every individual of our group willing to contribute in order to meet our timeline and devising feasible and achievable ideas for our project idea. Another aspect was that we met requirements for the rubric by meticulously reading through it, ensuring that all parts of the assignment were completed well.

    At least one thing that we have learned about groups.

    The key things we learned during our time as a group was that by working in a team environment, helped us foster creativity, build up mutual trust and assisted us in learning ways to resolve problems by formulating a solution together as a group.
